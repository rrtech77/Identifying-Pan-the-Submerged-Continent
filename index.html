Chapter I

Atlantis

Plato (427–347 BC) was born in Athens to an upper-class family. The famous Athenian lawgiver Solon (c. 638–558 BC) — six generations removed — was an ancestor. After reforming Greece, Solon travelled to Heliopolis and, subsequently at Sais in 559 BC, received from the priests of Neith's temple an account of Atlantis. The priests told him the great disaster had taken place almost 10,000 years earlier.

This power came forth out of the Atlantic Ocean, for in those days the Atlantic was navigable; and there was an island situated in front of the straits which you call the columns of Heracles; the island was larger than Libya and Asia put together… But afterwards there occurred violent earthquakes and floods; and in a single day and night of rain all your warlike men in a body sank into the earth, and the island of Atlantis in like manner disappeared, and was sunk beneath the sea.

— Plato, Timaeus

For the Greeks of Solon's time, the Atlantic Ocean was a body of water that completely surrounded the world. The Atlantis hypothesis was taken up at length by Congressman Ignatius Donnelly in Atlantis: the Antediluvian World (1882). In Plato's day, Libya denoted all of North Africa west of Egypt; Asia stretched between Egypt in the west, the Caucuses in the north, Arabia in the south, and India in the east — suggesting a former landmass too big to fit in the Atlantic Ocean.

It is believed that Plato merged tales of the Minoans and Bronze Age Mediterranean island warriors with a pre-existing collective memory of a worldwide deluge that destroyed an ancient civilization. Both Homer's account of the Trojan War and Plato's description of Atlantis mention a fleet of 1,200 ships and the use of chariots and bronze weapons.

Chapter II

Lemuria

Though living modern lemurs are found only in Madagascar and several surrounding islands, fossil lemurs were found extending from Pakistan to Malaysia. In 1864 geologist Philip Sclater proposed that Madagascar and India had once been part of a larger continent — named "Lemuria" for its lemurs.

The Lemuria theory disappeared from conventional scientific consideration after the acceptance of plate tectonics. According to continental drift, Madagascar and India were once part of the same landmass, but plate movement caused India to break away millions of years ago. The original landmass broke apart — it did not sink beneath the sea.

Chapter III

Kumari Kandam

According to modern interpretations of classical Tamil texts, the Dravidians originally came from Kumari Kandam (50,000 BC), a legendary landmass located at the southern tip of India in the Indian Ocean that became submerged (16,000 BC) by successive floods — believed to have connected Madagascar, South India and Australia, covering most of the Indian Ocean.

Manu and the Flood

Arya legends attribute their origins to King Satyavrata of Dravida, who later became known as Manu — the first Arya and progenitor of all Aryas. Here too, a deluge of unimaginable proportions is described, sinking a vast region from India to Madagascar where King Manu may have had his capital.

Chapter IV

Ta Rua — the Empire of the Sun

Legends suggest Hawaii may be the mountain tops of the submerged continent of Ta Rua — the Empire of the Sun. A cataclysm is preserved in ancient Hawaiian chant:

"Born were noises bursting in the air, explosions from volcanoes that blew the earth to pieces. Ta Rua disintegrated and sank lower and lower into the eddying sea while the four winds howled from all directions. The earth quaked and quivered, shook and shivered, as volcanic explosions rent Ta Rua asunder…"

Before disappearing into the sea, Ta Rua is believed to have covered present-day Hawaii, New Zealand, Tahiti, Samoa, and Easter Island. Oahspe tells us Pan was west of America — the motherland from where its chosen survivors brought civilization.

Chapter V

The Lost Continent of Mu

The idea of a lost Pacific continent known as 'Mu' first appeared in the works of Augustus Le Plongeon (1825–1908), who claimed to have translated ancient Mayan writings showing survivors of a continent 'Mu' founded Maya civilization. Le Plongeon got the name "Mu" from Brasseur, who in 1864 mistranslated what was then called the Troano Codex using the de Landa alphabet.

Colonel James Churchward (1851–1936) published The Lost Continent of Mu Motherland of Man in 1926, aged 75. He described Mu as "the home to the Naacals," a civilization flourishing 50,000 BC that was "technologically more advanced than ours." He named and quoted Oahspe in his work, and its map of Pan would prove prescient.

Nan Madol — One of the Seven Cities of Mu

Churchward posited that the site of Nan Madol, on Pohnpei Island in Micronesia, was one of the seven cities of ancient Mu. The cyclopean ruins — once a ceremonial centre covering 11 square miles — consist of around 90 small artificial islands built up out of a lagoon, interlinked by tidal canals. These islands contain house foundations, sea walls, tunnels and burial vaults, all constructed entirely from prismatic basalt columns stacked crisscross like log cabins. These rocks weigh several tons on average, the largest weighing 25 tons.

Chapter VIII

The Babylonian Deluge & Epic of Gilgamesh

The Legend of the Deluge existed in both Sumerian and Babylonian by 2000 BC. The Sumerians regarded the Deluge as a historic event they could date, recording lists of kings who reigned before the Flood. The Babylonian versions state that the flood was caused by rain, but some passages suggest other destructive physical phenomena connected with the earth.

"The Bible says the Deluge came because 'all the fountains of the great deep were broken up,' and 'the flood-gates of heaven were opened' (Gen. vii, ii). The rain flood was joined by the waters of the sea. Later tradition asserts that when Noah had entered the Ark and the door was shut — 'the floodgates of the heavens were opened and the foundations of the earth were rent asunder' — and that 'the ocean, that great sea which surroundeth the whole world, poured forth its floods.'"

Gilgamish, horrified by his companion Enkidu's death, sought from his ancestor Uta-Napishtim the secret of immortality. The immortal told him the story of the Flood — a narrative that parallels Noah's account in extraordinary detail: divine warning, construction of a vessel, boarding of animals and family, the flood itself, and birds sent out to test for dry land.

Chapter IX

Studying the Ocean Floor

During the 1920s and 1930s, Felix Meinesz developed a unique gravimeter that could measure gravity in the stable environment of a submarine, revealing oceanic trenches as sites of downwelling in the solid Earth. The heroic phase of trench exploration culminated in the 1960 descent of the Trieste, setting an unbeatable world record by diving to the bottom of Challenger Deep.

Marie Tharp created the first detailed maps of the ocean floor around the globe based on sonar, plotting profiles based on data from soundings taken by Ewing and Heezen. When pieced together, a cleft running down the center with peaks on each side was noticed — Tharp thought it might be a rift valley.

Further Context

Nakbe, El Mirador, and the Tikal Frieze

Nakbe had established monumental architecture as early as the 8th century BC, with some platforms 59 ft high. Around 1200 BC villages were leveled and filled to serve as platform foundations for large new buildings, indicating that platform construction and monumental architecture were planned, simultaneous events. The most impressive building at Nakbe is a pyramid flanked by two large stucco masks and topped with three triadic style roofed structures. The Kan Causeway at Nakbe was 13 ft above ground level; one causeway connected Nakbe with El Mirador, and later in the Late Classic period Nakbe was connected to the Maya center of Calakmul.

Excavations at Nakbe recovered 23 stone tools used to cut limestone blocks — bifacial picks and axes, hammerstones, and flake cores made of chert. Long-handled picks cut the limestone from bedrock; stone axes cut and formed the block. Nakbe remained virtually abandoned for a thousand years, until some late Classic Maya reoccupied the site, leaving fine ceramics but no monuments of their own.

The Mayan Stone Carving and the Tikal Frieze Controversy

What the Carving Shows

This Mayan stone relief depicts a temple destroyed by an earthquake, a drowning man, an erupting volcano, tidal waves, a man escaping in a boat, and overall: a giant flood. The Debunking — Jason Colavito's Analysis

Supposedly the piece was cut from atop a Maya temple at Tikal and shipped to Berlin where it was destroyed during World War II. That's the story David Hatcher Childress tells in Lost Cities of Atlantis (1996), derived from Peter Tompkins. The only documentation is this single "photograph," which is almost certainly a drawing. It was published in 1939 in Atlantis: Mother of Empires by Robert Stacy-Judd, an English architect best known for the Mayan Revival architectural style.

After reviewing hundreds of Maler's photos, Colavito found the Stacy-Judd image lacks the fine-grain details of stonework expected in real photographs. It contains elements not seen in typical Maya art — including a crumbling pyramid and modern use of perspective — much in keeping with the Art Deco-inspired Mayan Revival style favoured by Stacy-Judd. The claim it was destroyed in WWII in Berlin can be traced back no further than Peter Tompkins, contradicting Stacy-Judd's own 1939 book, which never said anything about the frieze leaving the Americas.

It is believed the Frieze illustrates the reason why the Mayans left the mythical homeland of Tulan. Part 3 of the Popol Vuh tells of creation, migration, and first dawn. The Annals of the Cakchiquels identifies the legendary Tulan as the place from which all set out, stating that the Kaqchikel ancestors came to Tulan ch'aqa palow — "across the sea" — from r(i) uqajib'al q'ij — "where the sun descends, the west." Geology

Pacific Geology, Plate Tectonics and Pan

The continental shelf ends at the shelf break. Beyond it, the continental slope descends about 4°, grooved by submarine canyons, about 16 km wide, to a depth of about 2.4 km. Then the continental rise leads to the abyssal plain at about 4 km depth. The abyssal plains of the Atlantic appear smooth because abyssal hills are buried under thick continental sediment; in the Pacific, tens of thousands of unburied abyssal hills have been observed. Beyond the abyssal plain the ocean floor ascends toward the mid-ocean ridge — the Mid-Atlantic Ridge snakes down the middle of the Atlantic from the North Pole to Antarctica.

The Pacific Plate and Pan's Location

The Pacific Plate is the only oceanic tectonic plate without a continent on it. The Pacific Ring of Fire — a 40,000 km horseshoe shape — has 452 volcanoes and is home to over 75% of the world's active and dormant volcanoes. 90% of the world's earthquakes occur along the Ring of Fire. Japan lies on the cusp of the Pacific-Philippine-Eurasian triple plate junction, where complex interactions of three tectonic plates are unpredictable and loaded with potential activity.

"The Pacific contains the only tectonic plate without a continent on it and it is surrounded by the famous 'Ring of Fire'; geological activity… concentrated in distinct zones which are usually not located at the boundaries of continents and oceans."

— Tjeerd Andel, New Views on an Old Planet, pp.122 & 132

Japan's Tectonic Plates and Volcanic Arcs

Japan's volcanoes are part of five volcanic arcs meeting at a triple junction on Honshu. The Northeast Honshu Arc and Kurile Arc were formed by subduction of the Pacific Plate under the Eurasian Plate. The Izu-Bonin Arc results from subduction of the Pacific Plate under the Philippine Plate. The Southwest Honshu Arc and Ryukyu Arc were formed by subduction of the Philippine Plate under the Eurasian Plate.
